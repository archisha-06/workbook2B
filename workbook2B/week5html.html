My Workbook website progress

Overview of Week 5: Coding Home-page and illustrations

In Week 5, I started working on building my Workbook 2B website using HTML, CSS and P5.JS. I wanted to implement my understanding on P5.js in my website elements as well, so I decided to code all the illustrations and interactive elements

Home Page


I began by defining the overall aesthetic and color scheme of the website. I chose a tech-futuristic typeface to complement the theme and carefully positioned the initial text elements to establish a clear visual hierarchy and layout.

Adding trail on cursor



To give my website a more dynamic, digital personality, I created a custom cursor trail using p5.js. Every time the mouse moves, it leaves behind a crosshair-like grid that fades out gradually, creating a subtle techy shimmer across the page. I wanted something that felt responsive but not overwhelming — something that visually follows the user without being too literal.
Instead of using background(), I opted for clear() so the rest of the site stays visible underneath the canvas. Each “particle” is a simple grid made of intersecting lines with decreasing opacity — a low-key nod to classic sci-fi UI design. It’s also layered behind all site content using a negative z-index, making it feel integrated into the overall vibe of the site rather than just an animation floating on top.



Header Illustration



Before landing on the final design, I actually went through a few different ideas. I tried some more minimal sketches, experimented with bright colours, even played around with geometric shapes fading in and out — but none of them really clicked. They either felt too static or didn’t match the energy I wanted my site to give off.





Eventually, I built this — a glowing, rotating web of 3D particles using p5.js and WebGL. As soon as I saw it in motion, I knew this was it. It had the cyber feel I was after — like a floating neural network or a piece of interactive sci-fi architecture.
The sketch is made up of hundreds of particles randomly placed in 3D space. When they're close enough to each other, they connect with fine neon lines, forming an evolving network that rotates gently on the Y and X axis. The whole thing runs behind the header and adapts to the window size — which took some fiddling to get right, but was worth it.
What I like most is how it captures a sense of motion and curiosity without being overwhelming. It's just subtle enough to live behind the content but still gives the site that futuristic, slightly mysterious vibe.



3D Illustrations

To link each week of my progress, I created three rotating 3D shapes using p5.js — a box, a sphere, and a cone — each sitting in their own little canvas. I liked the idea of giving every week its own visual identity, and these shapes felt like a fun and abstract way to represent each phase.
Each one rotates around a different axis (Y for the cube, X for the sphere, and Z for the cone), which gives them a subtle sense of individuality while still feeling like part of a cohesive set. I chose vibrant neon colors on a dark background to give them a futuristic, digital look that fits with the rest of my site’s style.
I also added a hover effect using CSS — when you hover over a shape, it scales up slightly and glows more intensely. It’s a small interaction, but it adds personality and makes the whole page feel more alive.
That said, getting these sketches to align properly was not as straightforward as I expected. Each canvas had its own quirks with spacing and margins, especially since they were generated dynamically with WebGL. I spent quite a bit of time fiddling with layout and container styles to get them to sit nicely next to each other. It was one of those behind-the-scenes struggles that no one really sees, but it definitely tested my patience.
In the end, I’m glad I pushed through — the result feels clean, interactive, and uniquely mine.



After getting all the interactive sketches working, I spent some time thinking about how to bring everything together. I didn’t want the homepage to feel like just a random collection of experiments — I wanted it to have flow, to guide people through my work and give a clear sense of progression.
So I started by adding direct links underneath each sketch, leading to the separate weekly pages. This way, the sketches weren’t just visuals — they became clickable entry points into my process. I tried to keep the layout clean and the typography consistent, just so everything felt intentional and easy to navigate.
Finally, I added a looping background video. This was something I debated for a while — I didn’t want it to overpower the content, but I also felt like a bit of subtle motion could tie everything together nicely. I chose something minimal and ambient, and layered it behind the main content using CSS positioning. It really helped set a tone for the site — slightly futuristic, a bit reflective, and still grounded in the work.
This last stage made the biggest difference. Suddenly, all the separate elements started to feel like parts of a whole. The site felt more like a portfolio and less like a sketchbook — still playful, but with a sense of intention and clarity.
